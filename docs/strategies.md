# Teaching the Times Tables Through Number Sense
### A research-based pedagogical breakdown of every fact family (0–12)

This guide synthesizes Greg Tang's decomposition-first approach (*The Best of Times*, TangMath) with the broader research consensus on fact fluency — chiefly Kling & Bay-Williams (*Math Fact Fluency*, ASCD 2019; "Three Steps to Mastering Multiplication Facts," *Teaching Children Mathematics* 2015), Van de Walle et al. (*Teaching Developmentally*), Baroody (2006), and Thornton (1978). The striking thing is how well these sources agree: **facts should be learned as relationships, not isolated items**, and the strategies used to derive facts *are* the number sense — they're the same distributive/associative reasoning students will need for multi-digit multiplication, mental math, and algebra.

---

## Part 1: The Framework

### The three phases of fact mastery (Kling & Bay-Williams)

1. **Counting/modeling** — student builds 4 groups of 3 with counters or arrays and counts to find the total. Necessary, but not the goal.
2. **Deriving** — student uses a known fact to reason to an unknown one ("I know 5×8=40, so 6×8 is one more group of 8: 48"). *This is the phase most instruction skips — and it's the phase where number sense is built.*
3. **Mastery/automaticity** — recall becomes automatic *as an outcome of* repeated strategic reasoning, not as a substitute for it.

Research is blunt about the alternative: jumping from counting straight to rote memorization produces brittle knowledge, inefficient fallback strategies, and math anxiety (Baroody 2006; Boaler 2014 on timed testing). Memorization is what happens *to* facts you've reasoned about many times.

### Foundational facts vs. derived facts

The single most important structural idea: **do not teach the tables in numerical order (0,1,2,3,4...).** Instead, teach a small set of *foundational* fact families first, because every other family can be derived from them:

**Foundational:** 2s → 10s → 5s → 0s & 1s → squares
**Derived:** 4s, 3s, 9s (moderate) → 6s, 8s, 7s, 11s, 12s (advanced)

Greg Tang's sequence in his *Mastering the Times Tables* series is nearly identical: easy (0, 1, 2, 10) → moderate (3, 4, 5, 9) → advanced (6, 7, 8). The logic is a "coverage" argument (Thornton 1978): easy, widely-applicable strategies get taught first and knock out most of the grid, so the harder strategies only have to carry a handful of stubborn facts.

### Two force multipliers

- **Commutativity (the "turn-around" rule).** Taught early through arrays (rotate a 3×7 array 90° and it's a 7×3 array), it instantly halves the learning load: 121 facts in a 0–10 grid collapse to 66 unique ones. By the time a student reaches the 7s, nearly every 7s fact is already known from earlier families.
- **The distributive property in disguise.** Every "break apart" strategy below (7n = 5n + 2n) is the distributive property. Naming this explicitly — Tang calls it "smart grouping," and it's literally partial products — is what makes these strategies transfer to 14 × 7 and eventually to algebra. This is the number-sense payoff: students learn that *numbers can be decomposed and recomposed at will.*

### Representations that carry the strategies

Strategies must be *seen*, not just told. The research-backed core toolkit:

- **Arrays and area models** — the master representation. A 6×7 array with a line drawn after the 5th row makes 6×7 = 5×7 + 1×7 visible. Area models scale directly to multi-digit multiplication and polynomial multiplication later.
- **Number bonds on factors** — decomposing the factor itself (7 → 5 and 2) before multiplying. This is the multiplication analogue of the number bonds students used for making-ten in addition.
- **Number lines** — support early phases and connect multiplication to measurement.
- **Number talks** — short daily discussions ("How could 6×6 help you with 6×7?") that make derivation the classroom norm.

## Part 2: The Table-by-Table Breakdown

Listed in **recommended teaching order**, with the primary strategy, the decomposition it rests on, and the number-sense concept it builds.

### Tier 1 — Foundational

**×2 — Doubling.**
Strategy: 2×n = n + n. Connect directly to the addition doubles students already know.
Number sense built: the double as a fundamental number relationship; the bridge from additive to multiplicative thinking. Tang: "Two is very fast and fun, quickly double and you're done."
*Doubling is the engine of this entire system — mastering it unlocks 4s, 8s, 3s, and 6s.*

**×10 — Place-value shift.**
Strategy: 10×n means n *tens*, so the digit shifts one place left. **Teach the why, not the trick**: "add a zero" fails at 10×3.5 and hides place value. Frame it as "4 groups of ten = 4 in the tens place."
Number sense built: base-ten structure — arguably the most important idea in elementary math. Anchors the 5s, 9s, 11s, and 12s.

**×5 — Half of ten.**
Strategy: 5×n = (10×n) ÷ 2. Also: the clock connection (5-minute marks) and the parity pattern (products end in 0 or 5).
Number sense built: halving/doubling relationships between factors; the 5-and-10 benchmark system that underpins mental arithmetic.
Bonus insight for even factors: 5×8 = 10×4 (halve one factor, double the other — the associative property in action).

**×1 and ×0 — Identity and zero.**
Strategy: reasoning about *meaning*: 1 group of n is n; 0 groups of anything is nothing. Teach these *after* 2s/10s/5s so multiplication already has meaning — teaching them first invites shallow rule-memorizing ("anything times 0 is 0") without understanding, and students overgeneralize (n+0, n×1 confusions).
Number sense built: identity and zero properties; attention to the *meaning* of the operation.

**Squares (n×n) — The diagonal anchors.**
Strategy: direct focused learning, supported by the visual of literal squares (growing square arrays) and their geometric beauty.
Number sense built: square numbers as a landmark set. Crucially, the hardest facts in the whole grid (6×7, 7×8, 6×8) all sit *next to* squares, so knowing the diagonal sets up the near-squares strategy.

### Tier 2 — First derived families

**×4 — Double-double.**
Decomposition: 4n = 2×(2n). Find 4×7: double 7 → 14, double again → 28.
Number sense built: associativity (4 = 2×2, so ×4 = ×2 then ×2); multiplicative structure of numbers. Tang: "please just always double twice!"

**×3 — Double plus one more group.**
Decomposition: 3n = 2n + n. Find 3×8: 16 + 8 = 24. (Tang's version: "times 2, then add one more.")
Number sense built: the "add a group" concept — the distributive property's simplest form, and the template for the 6s strategy. Emphasize adding one *group*, not one.

**×9 — Ten minus a group.**
Decomposition: 9n = 10n − n. Find 9×7: 70 − 7 = 63. Tang: "do 10 times and then subtract!"
Number sense built: **compensation** — overshooting to a friendly number and adjusting back. This is the strategy adults use for 99×anything and one of the deepest mental-math habits you can install.
Patterns as *verification*, not primary strategy: digits of 9s products sum to 9; tens digit is one less than the factor. The finger trick is fine as a curiosity but doesn't generalize (Kling & Bay-Williams caution against tricks-first).

### Tier 3 — Advanced derived families

**×6 — Five and one more group** (or double the 3s).
Decomposition A: 6n = 5n + n. Find 6×8: 40 + 8 = 48.
Decomposition B: 6n = 2×(3n). Find 6×7: 21 doubled = 42. Tang: "just multiply by 3 then 2."
Number sense built: strategy *choice* — students compare routes and pick based on the numbers, the very definition of flexibility. The 5s-anchor version reinforces the benchmark-5 structure (visible on rekenreks, ten-frames, dice patterns).

**×8 — Double-double-double** (or ten minus two groups).
Decomposition A: 8n = 2×2×2×n. Find 8×6: 12 → 24 → 48. Tang: "doubling three times works just great!"
Decomposition B: 8n = 10n − 2n. Find 8×7: 70 − 14 = 56.
Number sense built: exponential doubling structure; a second exposure to compensation. Again, having two routes is a feature — number talks comparing them are gold.

**×7 — The last one standing.**
Here's the quiet secret: by this point, **there is only one genuinely new fact** — 7×7 — and it's a square. Every other 7s fact is known via commutativity (7×4 = 4×7, already mastered with double-double). This is why sequencing matters: the "hardest" table arrives almost pre-learned, which is a massive motivation and math-anxiety win.
For direct derivation anyway: 7n = 5n + 2n (Tang: "first times 5 then add times 2") — the flagship break-apart fact, worth modeling on an array split into a 5-row band and a 2-row band.
Number sense built: full distributive decomposition using two benchmark anchors; the realization that a "hard" number is just easy numbers in a trench coat.

**Near squares — targeting the stubborn trio.**
The consistently hardest facts are 6×7, 7×8, and 6×8. Attack them with:
- 6×7 = 6×6 + 6 = 42 (one more group than a square)
- 7×8 = 7×7 + 7 = 56 (mnemonic backup: 5,6,7,8 → 56 = 7×8)
- 6×8 = 7×7 − 1 = 48 (a difference-of-squares glimpse: (n−1)(n+1) = n² − 1) or double 3×8.
Number sense built: n(n+1) = n² + n foreshadows quadratic structure; genuinely proto-algebraic reasoning.

### Tier 4 — Extending beyond 10 (11s and 12s)

**×11 — Ten plus a group.**
Decomposition: 11n = 10n + n. The repeating-digit pattern (11, 22, 33...) is a *consequence*, and asking students *why* it happens (4 tens + 4 ones) is a lovely place-value discussion. The pattern breaks at 11×10; the decomposition doesn't — which is exactly the lesson.

**×12 — Ten plus double.**
Decomposition: 12n = 10n + 2n. Find 12×7: 70 + 14 = 84.
Number sense built: this *is* two-digit multiplication. 12×7 decomposed this way is identical in structure to 23×7 or 145×7 via partial products. The 12s are the bridge out of the times tables entirely — Tang's "Beyond the Basic Times Tables" makes exactly this move.

## Part 3: Quick-Reference Strategy Map

| Family | Primary decomposition | Backup route | Core concept |
|---|---|---|---|
| ×0 | 0 groups = 0 | — | zero property |
| ×1 | 1 group of n = n | — | identity |
| ×2 | n + n | addition doubles | doubling |
| ×3 | 2n + n | — | add a group (distributive) |
| ×4 | double twice | — | associativity |
| ×5 | half of 10n | clock connection | benchmark 5 & 10 |
| ×6 | 5n + n | double the 3s | strategy choice |
| ×7 | 5n + 2n | commutativity covers nearly all | full break-apart |
| ×8 | double 3 times | 10n − 2n | doubling chains / compensation |
| ×9 | 10n − n | digit patterns (verify) | compensation |
| ×10 | place-value shift | — | base ten |
| ×11 | 10n + n | digit pattern (≤9 only) | place value |
| ×12 | 10n + 2n | — | partial products |
| n×n | learn as anchor set | square arrays | landmarks |
| near-squares | n² ± n | — | proto-algebra |

**Recommended teaching sequence:** 2 → 10 → 5 → 0/1 → squares → 4 → 3 → 9 → 6 → 8 → 7 → 11 → 12. Shade a multiplication chart as each family is mastered — commutativity means the grid fills startlingly fast, which students find genuinely motivating.

## Part 4: Practice Principles (what the research says about *how*)

**Do:**
- **Games over drills.** Kling & Bay-Williams' entire book is built on games because they generate high-volume, low-stress, strategy-rich repetition. Tang's site (tangmath.com) works the same way — Kakooma, Break Apart, Expresso all train decomposition itself, not just answers.
- **Number talks / strategy discussions.** "How could 5×8 help you with 6×8?" Make derivation the daily norm; have students defend and compare routes.
- **Represent every strategy visually** before abstracting it — array with a split line, then equation.
- **Assess by interview and observation**, not just answers: "Did you just know it, or use a strategy? Which one?" Fluency = accuracy + efficiency + *flexibility*.
- **Quarantine each strategy briefly** (Tang's approach): practice one decomposition until secure, *then* open up choice.

**Avoid:**
- **Timed tests as the primary tool.** They push students back toward fast-but-weak strategies (counting), cause math anxiety, and measure recall speed rather than fluency (Boaler; Kling & Bay-Williams).
- **Teaching 0s and 1s first**, or teaching families in numerical order — it frames facts as isolated objects.
- **Tricks without reasons** ("just add a zero," the 9s finger trick as the *only* route). Fine as garnish; corrosive as the meal.
- **Skipping the deriving phase** and going straight from counting to flashcards — the well-replicated failure mode.

## Part 5: Why this builds number sense (the payoff)

Every strategy above is a general principle wearing a specific fact's clothing:

- Doubling/halving → the associative property and factor manipulation (5×16 = 10×8)
- Add/subtract a group → the distributive property → partial products → the standard algorithm *with understanding* → binomial expansion
- Compensation (9s, 8s) → mental math with any near-friendly numbers (99×34, 4.9×6)
- Benchmark 5s and 10s → estimation and magnitude sense
- Near squares → n² ± n and (n−1)(n+1) = n² − 1 → algebraic identities

A student who "knows their tables" this way doesn't just have 144 stored answers — they have a demonstrated, practiced belief that **numbers are compositions that can be taken apart and rebuilt**, which is the working definition of number sense.
